Conclusion

The introduction of new anti-piracy laws and initiatives has not been responsible for a decrease in the number of adolescents illegally viewing copyrighted content in Australia. Any new laws and initiatives have proven ineffective in deterring any noticeable number of active pirates. This result is not due to the growth of any new streaming services, but rather the ineffectiveness of said anti-piracy laws and initiatives.

We are only seeing a modest 6.7% decrease in the number of active pirates. While a decrease none the less, it cannot be attributed to any new anti-piracy laws or initiatives. This is because of the 57% of adolescents surveyed who were aware of changes in government policy, and the 13% who had noticed any new anti-piracy campaigns/initiatives or adverts, none had reported that they stopped pirating content as a result. The most likely cause of this decrease is the growth of legal streaming services acknowledged by 3 separate reports over the last 3 years. Legal streaming among Australian pirates aged 12-17 has grown by 28% from 2014-2016 (Sycamore, 2016). Between 2014 and 2015 Choice put the average increase in subscriptions among Australians at 19% (Choice, 2015). Project rebel (adults) also put subscription of streaming services, among pirates aged 18-64 at 32% (in 2015) (Meadows, 2016).

Streaming of pirated content is on the rise but this is only in volume. Active pirates are starting to stream more and download less. At this point in time though active pirates are still downloading more than they stream. This growth is increasing piracy rates but only by 1.07%. This increase while small is enough to cancel out any effects of new anti-piracy laws and initiatives on the number of active pirates in Australia. It is clearly the ineffectiveness of the new anti-piracy laws and initiatives in the first place though that is the biggest reason for this, not growth in the streaming of pirated content.
