Key Findings

This report examined a variety ofqualitative and quantitative data, sourced from primary and secondary sources. The report shows the following: habits of pirates, trends in the streaming of pirated content, and the coherence of my report to other studies.

86.11% of adolescents who filled out our survey admitted to streaming or downloading pirated content in the last 12 months. This was much higher than expected. While the percentage of active pirates in this age group is typically quite large, The Department Of Communications Report found the number of people who consumed illegal content aged 16-24 was only 59% (DEPARTMENT OF COMMUNICATIONS[DOCR 2015], 2015, p. 33). Data collected from our survey of the age range 16-24 shows 91.95% of participants as active pirates. While DOCR 2015 time frame for active pirate classification was only 3 months it does not seem to account for the almost 33% difference in our findings. The only conclusion that can be made is that as a result of the limited demogrphic than the national average(2015).

Over 57% of adolescents surveyed said that they were aware of changes in Australian Government policy in the last year related to piracy. However, when asked what effect this had on the amount of content they consume, 0% said that they stopped pirating content. 72% said that the policy changes they were aware of had had no impact on the amount of pirated content they consume. This conforms with the results of project Rebel’s 4th most popular response when it surveyed 369 pirates about the reason they still piratem, “nobody is stopping me” (Sycamore, 2016, p. 7; Meadows, 2016, p. 31). This said though there is a minor decrease in the volume content people pirate.

Of 108 adolescents surveyed, only 13% had noticed any new anti-piracy campaigns/initiatives or adverts. Of the 3 that stated an effect, only one took action, which was prompted by an email from his ISP “about piracy” this action, however, did not affect the amount of content he pirates. This is similar to the recommendations of 6% of Australians asked about piracy (Meadows, 2016, p. 16). There is agreement that piracy is wrong though. In 2016 it was at its highest of 73%, this is up from 64% in 2014 (Meadows, 2016, p. 13). The reason for this shift is not clear, however it does not seem to have affected actual piracy numbers.

Out of the 11 non-active pirates, none had listed threat of persecution, new anti-piracy legislation, or initiatives as their reason for stopping. The majority of non-active Pirates stated that piracy is no longer as convenient. Others simply didn’t need to anymore due to change in economic situation or lack of interest. This is very similar to the results of campaign and initiative effects.

Overall there is a small decline in the number of active pirates with a net 6.7% decrease in active pirates. 11 have become non-active and only 4 have begun pirating.

When asked if over the last 2 years if it had become harder to pirate content, 76% of pirates said it had not, while 24% of pirates said it had. When asked how easy is it to currently obtain copyright material from a scale of 1(very hard)-10(very easy), the average for the same 24% was an 8. As a result it cannot have become significantly harder to pirate content over the last 2 years.

Over the last year streaming has increased, while downloading has decreased slightly. Despite the findings of the report, habits of the majority of pirates have not changed drastically. Today downloading still surpasses streaming as people’s primary way of pirating content. Streaming has increased by almost 13% in 2 years.
